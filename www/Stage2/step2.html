Emotions, love ‘em or hate ‘em, we’re stuck with ‘em. It probably feels ok when you’re having feelings that you like, like excitement, but if it’s actually the case that you are having to deal with the more difficult emotions of (what students input in stage 1, step 2, page? ) then what does that mean:-- gritting your teeth until it passes?

No, it’s rather like the pink elephant exercise, the more you try to escape, avoid, suppress, and ignore your feelings, the longer they stick around, and the worse it feels.
Remember Yolanda? She felt anxious about looking at her timetable and so stopped looking at it. This helped her to avoid the initial anxiety but resulted in later feelings of guilt and heightened anxiety.

Take a moment to notice the emotions you experience when thinking about the things that you have to do but are putting off. You'll have a minute to identify the emotions. Here's a reminder of the 4 broad categories that our emotions can fall in to.

Anger Joy
Sadness Fear

Whether you manage to sit with the emotion without struggling with it for the full minute or not this step is about building tolerance for whatever emotions turn up for you, whether it is a pleasant (but unhelpful) feeling of disinterest in your work or an unpleasant feeling of panic about all the work you have to do.

Tolerance is like going to the gym to develop strength and stamina in your muscles. You can’t lift 50 kilos of weight on your first visit to the gym, you have to work up to it steadily over time. This step also requires willingness to have, or allow yourself, to feel feelings. Willingness doesn’t mean liking or wanting the feeling , but allowing it to be there.
